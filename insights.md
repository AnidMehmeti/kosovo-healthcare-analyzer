Nearly half of Kosovo's analyzed population lives in municipalities critically underserved by primary healthcare, concentrated in a red band around the capital, where Prishtinë's gravitational pull on private investment has left larger regional cities without sufficient infrastructure to serve both their own residents and the smaller municipalities that depend on them.
This analysis covers 27 municipalities representing 95.5% of Kosovo's population, using 2024 population estimates from the Kosovo Agency of Statistics and facility data from OpenStreetMap. Primary care facilities include hospitals, clinics, and doctors' offices. Note that recent private clinic developments in cities like Mitrovica and Ferizaj may not be fully captured in this dataset.
The first finding of this analysis is that 46.8% of the analyzed population (708,685 residents) lives in municipalities below the 0.162 baseline, meaning that there is a geographical inequality when it comes to primary care availability per municipality. Post-war private investment concentrated in Prishtinë, leaving municipalities in its proximity underserved despite their large populations.
This directly relates to the second finding which I refer to as the "Prishtina black hole" effect. The larger municipalities closer to Prishtina (e.g., Mitrovica, Ferizaj, Podujeve) have a gravitational pull towards Prishtina's primary care services, resulting in less services within their own municipalities. Further, smaller municipalities around Prishtina suffer from this same effect, potentially leaving their residents to get primary care in the capital instead of their respective municipalities. As discussed above, larger municipalities in Western Kosovo (e.g., Gjakova, Peje, Prizren) are above the baseline and create a gravitational hole towards smaller municipalities closer to them, mimicking Prishtina's "black hole" effect on a smaller scale.
The third finding relates to this "gravitational pull" larger municipalities have on neighboring, smaller municipalities. If we were to observe the situation in Mitrovica and Ferizaj, we can predict that an investment in both cities would significantly improve the situation for smaller municipalities close to them, where their residents could choose primary care in these larger municipalities instead of their own, trickling down the effect a potential investment in Mitrovica or Ferizaj might have on municipalities like Skenderaj, Viti, Shtime, Suhareke or Kline. This prediction is supported by the recent agreement between the Office of the President of Kosovo, Vjosa Osmani, and a delegate of the United Arab Emirates for a UAE-funded regional hospital in Mitrovica. This addresses not only the situation in a large municipality like Mitrovica, but also benefits others around it as well, potentially helping to balance out the large crowd and concentration of primary care currently in Prishtina.
However, we have to address the limitations of this current analysis. Of the 11 excluded municipalities, 8 have a Serb-majority population (Graçanicë, Kllokot, Mitrovica e V., Partesh, Ranillug, Novobërdë, Shtërpcë, Zubin Potok). The remaining 3 (Hani i Elezit, Junik, and Mamushë) were excluded solely due to missing shapefile boundary data. Also, there is a gap regarding recent private clinic data, which might "soften up" the gap from the baseline for larger municipalities like Mitrovica and Ferizaj if they were available and taken into account for this analysis. A complete picture of healthcare access in Kosovo cannot be drawn without data from the excluded municipalities, which represents a significant gap for any national health policy.
A concrete policy recommendation is to invest in larger municipalities around Prishtina, such as Mitrovica (already in-progress) and Ferizaj, which will significantly benefit the "traffic load" of primary care in Prishtina, while simulatenously helping smaller municipalities get primary care closer to their home municipality instead of heading immediately to the capital.
